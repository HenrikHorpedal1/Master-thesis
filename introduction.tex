\section{History of the Leslie speaker}
The Leslie speaker was invented by Donald Leslie between 1937 and 1940 in Los Angeles, California \cite{vail2002}. His inspiration came after attending a demonstration of the then-new Hammond Organ, which impressed him greatly in the showroom but left him disappointed when he tried it at home. The demonstration had taken place in a long, narrow room with excellent acoustics, conditions that the instrument could not replicate in a domestic setting.

What Leslie found himself missing was the sense of motion inherent to pipe and theatre organs. In those instruments, the pipes are spread across a room --- often spanning 12 feet or more --- so that as different notes are played, the apparent source of the sound shifts. Wanting to bring that same spatial quality to his Hammond organ, Leslie began experimenting with rotating speakers.

In 1940, he founded his own company, Electro Music, to develop and commercialise the concept. He approached Hammond with the idea, but the company showed no interest; Laurence Hammond, in particular, was dismissive and actively discouraged the pairing of his organs with Leslie's cabinet. The two companies remained at odds for decades, until Hammond eventually acquired Electro Music in 1980, ending the long-running feud \citep{motleymoose2015hammondleslie}.

\subsection{Famous songs that use the Leslie sound effect}
This section is taken in its entirety from \cite{horpedal2025projectthesis}.

Although inseparable from the Hammond organ in many listeners' minds, Leslie and Leslie-style speakers have been used with instruments other than organ. To further motivate this work, here are four handpicked famous songs that showcase the diversity and influence of the Leslie speaker.

\textbf{``Black Hole Sun'' --- Soundgarden}\\
Released in May 1994 by the grunge band Soundgarden, ``Black Hole Sun'' features a very distinct guitar sound entering at \textbf{00:16}. The band's lead guitarist confirmed that they used a Leslie speaker in an interview with \textit{Guitar World} in 1996 \citep{KittsTolinskiSteinblatt1998NirvanaGrungeRevolution}. Other sources state that the model was a Leslie 16, a model designed specifically for guitar \citep{Gill2019CornellToneBlackHoleSun}.

\textbf{``Echoes'' --- Pink Floyd}\\
``Echoes'' by Pink Floyd was released on the album \textit{Meddle} in 1971. The intro features a repeating high-pitched note, like an eerie sonar ping. This sound was achieved by recording a piano and later playing the recording through a Leslie cabinet \citep{Povey2007PigsMightFly}.

\textbf{``Little Wing'' --- Jimi Hendrix}\\
``Little Wing'' was released in 1967 on the album \textit{Axis: Bold as Love}. When mixing the song, Eddie Kramer fed the guitar signal through a homemade Leslie \citep{McDermottKramerCox2009UltimateHendrix}. The effect can be heard entering the song at \textbf{00:23}.

\textbf{``Tomorrow Never Knows'' --- The Beatles}\\
On this track, John Lennon wanted his voice to sound like the Dalai Lama. The sound engineer Geoff Emerick suggested recording Lennon's vocal and re-recording it through a Leslie speaker, which is what can be heard on ``Tomorrow Never Knows'' \citep{Beatles2000Anthology}.
